ABSTRACT

Keywords:
Lithosphere- ionosphere mechanism
Earthquakes
GNSS TEC
Geomagnetic storm

Statistical analyses on the intensification of seismic precursors before and after the earthquakes (EQs) from
Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) based Total Electron Content (TEC) may provide evidences about the
potential mechanisms of lithosphere-ionosphere coupling. In this paper, ionospheric anomalies before and after
1182 (Mw > 5.0) EQs during 1998–2019 at three different latitudinal zones (80°N–25°N, 25°N–25°S, and
25°S–80°S) are investigated from global GNSS TEC observations. Previous studies proposed different time intervals for the occurrence of TEC anomalies triggered by EQs. In this study, we reveal that prominent TEC
anomalies in the form of enhancement and depletion occur within 5 days before and after the main shock of the
EQs. Further analysis suggests that the probability of TEC anomalies occurred before EQs of Mw ≥ 6.0 and focal
depth of less than 220 km (Zone B) is about 0.8. Similarly, a probability of 0.65 for TEC anomalies is recorded
before EQs of Mw ≥ 7.0 and focal depth less than 120 km in Zones-A and C. In our analysis, TEC anomalies
generated by EQs are isolated from those produced by the geomagnetic storms through the Kp geomagnetic
activity index and the disturbance storm time (Dst) index.

1. Introduction
The existence of lithosphere-ionosphere coupling before and after
strong EQs is an intriguing phenomena still not understood which has
been widely reported in the literature [1–3]. Among these studies,
anomalous EQ perturbation from statistical analysis have shown clear
insights on the generation of ionospheric perturbations before and after
strong EQs [4–6]. Moreover, ionospheric anomalies are discussed in
details from the analysis of ground and space measurements [7]. Liu
et al. [8] first implemented a statistical approach with TEC estimations
over the epicenter of the 1999 Chi-Chi event, and found significant
perturbations in the ionosphere. Later on, a number of statistical studies
have reported abnormal behavior of ionospheric TEC subject to EQs
[9,10], and the modeling of ionospheric data has been tested for the
prediction of EQs [11]. In all these studies, the statistical analyses have
provided a background to study the occurrence of ionospheric
∗

anomalies as implemented by Liu et al. [12]. Similarly, ionospheric
anomalies associated with large EQs in the form of statistical analysis
have been reported from the Detection of Electromagnetic Emissions
Transmitted from Earthquake Regions (DEMETER) satellite measurements in Shah et al. [13].
With the development of Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS)
technologies and its application in atmospheric sciences, specifically
TEC retrieval, more and more studies have reported the enhancement of
TEC before and after the main shock. Similarly, other reports have
shown the anomalous enhancement in TEC during the EQ preparation
period [14]. Moreover, Liu et al. [12] observed the anomalous increase
in TEC in a statistical analysis performed for EQs with Mw ≥ 5.0 during
1994–1999 in Taiwan, and determined that TEC perturbations could
only be seen for large magnitude and shallow depths EQs. They further
emphasized on the importance of statistical analysis for possible links of
TEC anomalies over the EQ breeding zone. Then, many researchers

anomalies around the seismically active regions on Earth for successful

Since geomagnetic storms can also cause TEC anomalies, several
studies have associated TEC variations to geomagnetic storms instead of
EQs [22,29–31]. These reports stressed on the inheritance of TEC
anomalies with geomagnetic storms, and specifically declared that
geomagnetic storms induced reasonable TEC variations as compared to
EQs. Therefore, it is necessary to remove from the statistical analysis
those days associated with geomagnetic storms, so we exclude the days
with Dst < −50 nT and Kp > 3. Geomagnetic storms usually have
three phases, namely initial, main and recovery phases [32,33]. Then,
we exclude the days which comprise the initial to the recovery phase of
the geomagnetic storm. Similarly, abnormal TEC days beyond 10 days
before and 10 after the EQs or geomagnetic storms are not counted in
the statistical analysis. This scheme may provide a clear picture of the
TEC variation triggered by either EQs or geomagnetic storm within 10
days before/after. As this study mainly focus on possible anomalies
within the time interval of 10 days before and after the main shock.

2. Data and method
The statistical analysis of seismo ionospheric anomalies by the enhancement and depletion of TEC related to 1182 EQs of Mw > 5.0
during 1998–2019 is divided in three different latitudinal zones
(80°N–25°N, 25°N–25°S, and 25°S–80°S). In this study, the ionospheric
perturbations and its different morphological characteristics related to
EQs are investigated before and after the seismic events. The different
morphological parameters of the EQs include magnitude, depth, and
location, and the data is retrieved from the website of the United States
Geological Survey (USGS) http://earthquake.usgs.gov/earthquakes/
search/. The 1182 EQs included in this study are declustered from
aftershocks following the method of Michael [23] and Thomas et al.
[5]. The resulting geographical location of the declustered epicenters is
shown in Fig. 1.
Along with different EQ parameters, we have obtained space
weather conditions for each event. We consider the effects of geomagnetic storms in TEC variability and employ the geomagnetic storm
activity index (Kp) and the disturbance storm time index (Dst) available
at the OMNI web NASA and the Kyoto University websites (http://wdc.
kugi.kyoto-u.ac.jp/dstdir/index.html). Fig. 2 shows an example of the
magnetospheric conditions before and after the February 21, 2017,
Bolivia EQ. Therefore, the 1182 EQs employed in this study have been
screened depending on the geomagnetic storm conditions.
We employ hourly TEC combined product from the International
GNSS Service (IGS) [24–26], to extract the vertical TEC (VTEC) at the
location of each EQ epicenter. Each VTEC map comprises a longitudinal
extension of ± 180° and a latitudinal extension of ± 87.5° [37]. The
grids are provided at 5.0° × 2.5° resolution in longitude and latitude,
respectively [38].

3. Results and discussions
In order to quantify the seismo ionospheric anomalies, we first investigate as a case study the EQ on February 21, 2017 (Mw 6.5, Padilla
Bolivia). The geographical location of its epicenter is 19.281°S and
63.905°W. This EQ hits the region at 17:09:04 (UTC) at a hypocentral
depth of 50 km. The epicenter was located 41 km East of Padilla city,
Bolivia, and occurred in the Zone-B of the study area. Fig. 3 shows the
diurnal TEC for 31 days before and after the main shock, with the
corresponding confidence bounds. A significant positive anomaly is
seen over the epicenter on the day of the main shock. It can be noted
here that we only count TEC anomalies within 10 days before and after
the main shock of this EQ. Similar analysis is implemented for other
1182 EQs of Mw > 5.0. To provide a stringent proof of temporal TEC
variations triggered by the Mw 6.5 Bolivia EQ, spatial TEC deviation is
plotted over the epicenter in Fig. 4. The deviation in spatial TEC is
obtained by subtracting the monthly TEC values from the observed TEC
at a specific UT hour. We can see that the temporal plot in Fig. 3 has a
positive anomaly on the EQ day for more than 4 h. TEC values larger
than 10 TECU are also noted in the spatial maps during UT = 10–15 h
over the EQ epicenter (Fig. 4). The deviation in TEC values over the
epicenter is greater than 30% during these hours, relatively larger than
the rest of the analyzed period.

are recorded before and after the different storms, and the abnormal
days associated with these geomagnetic storm are excluded from the

purpose of the study of TEC anomalies due geomagnetic storms is to
determine the effect of space weather environment on the ionosphere.
Furthermore it also assists in differentiating EQ and storm anomalies.
There are several reports about the abnormal TEC values in the ionosphere related to geomagnetic storms [21,29,35]. However, the contribution in TEC anomalies due to EQs is more prominent in past reports
[4,8,12].
In this paper, a statistical analysis of seismo ionospheric anomalies
related to Mw > 5 EQs is performed in three different latitudinal zones
to determine the morphological characteristics of the TEC anomalies as
a result of EQ intensification. The three zones include the equatorial
zone (Zone-B) and the mid-to-low/high latitude zones (Zones-A and C).
The enhancement and depletion in TEC due to equatorial and nonequatorial anomaly is correlated with EQs, since EQs trigger enhancements and depletion at different latitudes [12,13,36]. In this paper, we
have investigated EQs as a main driving force to anomalous TEC at the
equatorial and mid-to-low/high latitudinal regions.

Previous reports on seismo ionospheric anomalies in the equatorial
regions have reported ionospheric anomalies from TEC and other satellite measurements [13,15]. These authors showed the evidence for
seismo ionospheric anomalies before and after the EQs in the equatorial
and mid-latitudinal sectors, but no comprehensive statistical analysis of
large data set for different latitudinal regions were presented as we
have shown in this paper.
The goal of this study is to find the time interval for monitoring TEC
anomalies before and after the main shocks of EQs. As discussed above,
we have seen sharp perturbations within 5 days before and after the EQ
events, and severe TEC enhancements lasted even after the 5th day. The
evidence of seismo ionspheric anomalies within 5 days before and after
the main shock agrees with the findings of Liu et al. [12] and Kon et al.
[15]. These authors pointed out an abrupt TEC variations during 5 days
before the main shock, lasting few days after main shock.

4. Conclusions
In this paper, TEC intensification and depletion in the form of
seismo ionospheric anomalies prior to EQs have been studied for 1182
events globally during 1998–2019 for three different latitudinal zones.
We exclude variations caused by geomagnetic storms and that is estimated by a separate analysis. The results have shown evident existence
of TEC anomalies within 5 days before and after the EQs, being the preseismic ionospheric anomalies stronger than the post-seismic ionospheric anomalies. Furthermore, the EQs of Mw > 6.0 and focal
depth < 220 km caused TEC anomalies in the equatorial to low latitude
regions (Zone-B), whereas the EQs having Mw > 7.0 and focal
depth < 120 km can produce TEC anomalies in the North and South
mid to high latitudes (Zones-A and C). Moreover, we have found that
the percentage of occurrence of positive anomalies is larger than the
negative anomalies. Geomagnetic storms of high intensity caused more
variations than low intensity storms, as evident from TEC anomalies.
This study provides a deep insight into different morphological characteristics of TEC anomalies due to EQs; however, more work is required to investigate the phenomena of EQ forecasting.
